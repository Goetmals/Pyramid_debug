General FAQ

Q: What is the RapidIO interconnect architecture?

a The RapidIO interconnect architecture is a new electronic data communication standard for interconnecting chips on a circuit board and for interconnecting circuit boards using a backplane. The RapidIO standard increases performance and provides a more robust interface for future networking products and high performance embedded systems.

Q: What is the RapidIO interconnect target market?

A: The RapidIO interconnect is primarily targeted at the networking market. Unlike other contemporary computer-centric interconnects, the RapidIO technology addresses the networking industry's needs for software transparency, greater reliability, and higher bandwidth in an "in-the-box interconnect." The RapidIO interconnect provides higher bus speeds that allow chip-to-chip and board-to-board communications at performance levels scaling greater than ten gigabits per second.

Q: How is the RapidIO technology uniquely optimized for intra-system interconnections?

A: RapidIO technology provides a rich set of hardware features for high availability and configuration. One such example is the ability to detect and recover from transmission errors.

In its simplest form, a RapidIO end-point can fit inside an FPGA. RapidIO end-points are small enough to leave most of the FPGA available for other functionality. RapidIO technology is flexible, allowing a number of system topologies, address maps, and transactions to suit a variety of applications. RapidIO implementations can boast high throughput, deterministically low latency, low cost and low power. Designers can add multiple RapidIO ports to new I/O chips, providing the performance benefits of a fabric interconnect without incurring the costs associated with adding a dedicated switching chip.

For more information on this topic visit the Technology page.

Q: Where can I find the latest information on the RapidIO Trade Association and RapidIO interconnect architecture?

A: Visit the RapidIO Trade Association's web site at http://www.RapidIO.org/.

Architectural FAQ

Q: Where will I find the RapidIO interconnect architecture?

A: Integrated directly onto future microprocessors, digital signal processors, network processors, and communications processors. You will also find RapidIO technology used inside PCI-X and PCI fabrics. In more technical terms, the RapidIO technology is a high-performance, packet-switched, in-the-box interconnect.

Q: What does the RapidIO interconnect architecture look like?

A: RapidIO technology is a packet-switched protocol implemented on standard printed circuit board technology. Different parts of a RapidIO fabric can run at different rates. Thus, RapidIO interconnects have the potential to evolve over time to support other segments of the embedded market.

The first version of the RapidIO specification requires 40 pins per port with an 8-bit data path, or 76 pins per port with a 16-bit and is full duplex. The physical signaling is standard LVDS operating from 100 MHz to 1 GHz. Data is transferred with a source synchronous clock and sampled on both clock edges. The total bandwidth per port ranges from 3 gigabits to more than 60 gigabits per second.

To software, the RapidIO interconnect look like a traditional microprocessor and peripheral bus, so hardware implementations can hide things like discovery and error management from software, unless a software system elects to participate. This is another example of the RapidIO technology's inherent compatibility with legacy system and application software.

The RapidIO interconnect architecture also boasts an optional distributed globally shared memory protocol extension that is useful for symmetric multiprocessing and shared date structures.

Go to the Technology page for more information on the RapidIO Architecture.

Q: How is software development impacted by the RapidIO technology?

A: Software development is independent of the RapidIO interconnect architecture. RapidIO technology is transparent to the existing software base. To software, the RapidIO interconnect can look just like a traditional microprocessor and peripheral bus. RapidIO technology also bridges easily to PCI and PCI-X. These features allow users to mix legacy software and PCI chips with RapidIO chips, and there is no need for special device drivers.

Q: Will RapidIO technology force circuit designers to add switch chips into products that leverage the RapidIO interconnect?

A: There will be lots of choices. Peripheral chip designers will choose to implement the RapidIO interconnect in future versions of chips. For example, chips that bridge PCI into a proprietary microprocessor bus today will likely evolve to bridge PCI into a RapidIO interconnect, but include an embedded RapidIO switch on-chip as well.

Q: What features make RapidIO better than other interconnects?

A: The RapidIO Technology is very scalable in performance and overall system scalability. Here are just some of the advantages of RapidIO:

  • Performance: low overhead, low latency, optimized as an "inside-the-box," device-level interface
  • Functionality: packet-switched, point-to-point interconnect
  • Cost: can exist in a small silicon footprint, consumes few gates in FPGAs, uses few signal pins
  • Compatibility: software transparency, Reliability: error detection, recovery in hardware
  • Flexibility: layered architecture
  • Standard I/O: standard LVDS I/O technology

Competitive FAQ

Q: How does the RapidIO technology compare to InfiniBand?

A: InfiniBand is optimized for links between server chassis within an enterprise cluster to form a SAN. In contrast, the RapidIO technology replaces the traditional microprocessor and peripheral bus and is optimized for use between chips on a circuit board. You will likely find RapidIO interconnects co-existing inside InfiniBand subsystems. For example, the RapidIO technology might provide the bridge to PCI-X slots inside a single serer. You may also find the RapidIO technology providing concurrency and bandwidth aggregation inside the storage subsystem. We are quite confident that you will find the RapidIO technology inside networking devices that attach to InfiniBand.

Both InfiniBand and the RapidIO technology reach into the card-to-card communications domain. In this domain where we overlap, InfiniBand provides a more abstracted interface to allow complete decoupling of the subsystems. To accomplish this abstraction, InfiniBand requires modification of legacy software, more transistors to implement, and specialized management software.

Q: Specifically, how does the RapidIO technology compare to CSIX and IX?

A: CSIX is a hardware interface proposal that targets the networking data path and is used to interface devices to switch fabric in the data plane. In contrast, RapidIO targets the control plane. CSIX is not a protocol. The RapidIO protocol could evolve to support the CSIX interface. However, the RapidIO Trade Association is not currently working to do this.

IX is an Intel proprietary again targeted at the data path.

Q: How does the RapidIO technology compare to PCI and PCI-X?

A: The RapidIO architecture is designed to allow bridge chips to leverage the RapidIO technology inside PCI or PCI-X fabrics. Thanks to the RapidIO architecture, designers can now configure PCI-oriented systems containing hundreds of PCI or PCI-X slots. RapidIO aggregates PCI activity and then channels that activity to one or more processors.

Q: How does the RapidIO technology compare to Rambus?

A: In December 1999, Rambus announced their intent to introduce chip-to-chip interconnect products for the networking market. Rambus' technical strength is at the physical layer. It is theoretically possible that a future version of the RapidIO architecture could leverage a Rambus physical layer.

Q: How does the RapidIO technology compare to fabrics targeted for network data path switching?

A: The RapidIO architecture is primarily the evolution of the traditional microprocessor and peripheral bus. This rich history makes the RapidIO technology quite general purpose. With respect to the network market, the first version of the RapidIO architecture is optimized more for routers that mix control and data traffic onto a single bus --optimizations that also apply to the control side of routers that separate control and data traffic. The RapidIO architecture's initial quality of service feature set is adequate for many data path switching applications.

RapidIO Trade Association FAQ

Q: What is the RapidIO Trade Association (RTA)?

A: The RapidIO Trade Association is a 501(c)6 nonprofit organization, which directs future development and promotion of the interconnect as an open standard. It also encourages networking market participants to join and thereby participate in the future of the RapidIO interconnect standard.

The RapidIO architecture's community-oriented development process will produce a true open standard.

Q: Who are the members of the RTA?

A: To see an updated list of companies in the RTA, visit http://www.RapidIO.org/.

Q: What do I get by joining the RTA?

A: Members of the association will have early access to the RapidIO architecture specifications. Membership also enables attendance at member meetings, participation in the development of the standard, and a license to the IP. Visit our Membership Information page for more information.

Q: Are there fees associated with the RTA membership, and if so, how much are they?

A: Yes, the amount depends on the level of membership. Annual membership fees are $25,000 for the Steering Committee, $15,000 for Sponsoring Members, and $9500 for Regular Members. For more information please visit our Membership page.

Q: What are these fees used for?

A: Annual membership dues are associated with each level of membership and are set by the Steering Committee. These fees permit the association to adhere to the nonprofit 501(c)6 regulations. All fees are initially directed to the administration, legal and public relations of the association and are payable to the association by each class of member. The fee structure is subject to change by resolution of the Steering Committee.

Q: How s the RTA structured?

A: The RapidIO Trade Association is a 501(c)6 non-profit organization controlled by its members. The members have elected a Steering Committee, which in turn oversees a Technical Working Group (TWG) and a Marketing Working Group (MWG). Responsibilities of the TWG include providing a forum to coordinate specification development activities, manage the maintenance of the specification, and advising the TWG chair when amending policies and procedures. The MWG is responsible for all marketing and public communications activities for RapidIO, including press releases, trade show exhibitions, and conference presentations. The MWG also oversees the RapidIO web site, and plays the role of a Membership Committee for the RTA. Both the TWG and the MWG also coordinate the activities of various Task Groups. Visit our Organization page for more information.

Q: Is there a royalty associated with the RapidIO interconnect architecture?

A: The members of the Trade Association are committed both to an open architecture with minimal legal impediments to adoption and to a broadly accepted architecture. The Trade Association by-laws strike a balance between preserving its members intellectual property rights and preventing any one member from boxing out another member in the market place.

As a requirement to membership, each member agrees to license its essential patents to every other member under fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms. The RTA cannot guarantee that future members will never adopt specifications that require royalty payments.

After adoption, each specification will be made publicly available to members and to non-members alike. A modest fee to cover printing costs may be imposed by the RTA.
