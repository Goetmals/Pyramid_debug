RapidIO: Interconnect Architecture for Embedded Systems

by Richard Jaenicke, Mercury Computer Systems, Inc., Chelmsford, MA

Processor technology, advancing at Moore's Law rates, has created a gap between processor performance and communications performance. RapidIO™ is a new, high-performance interconnect that directly addresses the need for embedded systems. Its high bandwidth and low latency provide the required bandwidth to feed the fastest processors.

RapidIO is optimized for chip-to-chip and board-to-board communications within embedded computer systems. Targeted for an initial bandwidth of more than 1 gigabyte per second per device pair, RapidIO provides the data throughput required for increasingly complex applications running on ever-faster processors, and requiring ever more data to move between multiple processing stages. Just as important, it is easy to use and appears as a traditional microprocessor bus to the software.

The RapidIO Architecture

RapidIO is a logical intra-system complement to intersystem fabric initiatives such as InfiniBand. In effect, RapidIO links the components in a chassis to form a system, and InfiniBand connects systems in a system area network.

Mercury Computer Systems, Inc. and Motorola collaborated in developing and promoting the RapidIO switched-fabric architecture. The RapidIO specification results from a 1998 agreement between Mercury and Motorola Semiconductor Products Sector to merge their previously independent work on next-generation embedded interconnect fabrics.

RapidIO builds on the foundation of the industry-standard RACEway Interlink (ANSI/VITA 5-1994) architecture and takes switched-fabric interconnects to the next level of performance. It is implemented using crossbar switches, similar to today's switched-fabric architectures. As Figure 1 shows, one or two crossbars allow very efficient interconnection of a few RapidIO devices. A hierarchy of multiple crossbars, similar to those used in existing architectures such as the RACEway Interlink, would extend this capability to many devices.

Physically, RapidIO is a packet-based protocol implemented on a copper-based interconnect. The first version that will appear on the market requires 40 pins per port, providing a full duplex 8-bit data path using standard LVDS signaling.

RapidIO is targeted for clock rates from 250MHz to more than 1GHz. Data is sampled on both edges of the clock. The draft specification includes provisions for faster clock rates, as well as different data path widths. The resulting data rates in each direction scale to 2 giabytes per second per port for the 8-bit interface, and 4 gigabytes per second for the 16-bit interface. Thus RapidIO provides the foundation to meet the data transit demands of many generations of embedded processors.

These point-to-point data rates are further augmented by RapidIO's ability to form crossbar-based switched fabrics. This enables topologies that provide multiple paths within a chassis, and that scale directly with the number of crossbars in the hierarchy. The result is the ability to increase aggregate system bandwidth simply by adding additional, low-cost crossbar components, providing virtually unlimited bandwidth within a system.

Ease of Use

RapidIO has its origins in the microprocessor bus, and its direct memory-mapped address model is very easy to use. RapidIO supports multiple programming models, including NUMA with physical addresses, ccNUMA, and message passing. This permits simultaneous distributed I/O processing and general-purpose multiprocessing within a single system.

To software, RapidIO looks like a traditional microprocessor bus. Hardware implementations can handle issues like discovery and error management, which helps to protect users' legacy software investment.

RapidIO also bridges cleanly into PCI and PCI-X architectures, and some prospective members of the RapidIO Trade Association have simulated RapidIO operation over the user I/O pins described within the CompactPCI standard. A formal endorsement of a specific RapidIO mapping in the CompactPCI community is being pursued. Work is also ongoing to determine RapidIO's ability to transit the user pins on the VME P0 connector.

RapidIO in Embedded Systems

RapidIO is focused as a processor, memory and memory-mapped I/O interface with very little impact on embedded system software. RapidIO ports can be implemented directly on a microprocessor or in a small portion at a modern Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA). Within a system, these ports lead to a crossbar network, where the network can contain memory, I/O, and bridges to bus architectures.

RapidIO end points fit easily into an FPGA with plenty of room left over for application specific logic. This makes RapidIO easy to fit into the tight confines of embedded systems, and also results in lower costs compared to technologies that require dedicated componentry. One implementation consumes about 20,000 gates within an Altera FPGA and leading FPGA manufacturers are expected to add RapidIO end points to their libraries.

Similarly, peripheral chip designers may choose to embed RapidIO endpoints in a small corner of the silicon. For example, chips that today bridge PCI into a proprietary microprocessor bus will likely evolve to bridge PCI into RapidIO, and may include an embedded RapidIO switch as well. And where separate switch chips prove necessary, switches with multiple ports will likely bear prices similar to today's PCI bridging chips.

Figure 2 illustrates one powerful application of RapidIO in embedded systems. In this model, RapidIO crossbars form a virtual PCI bus for high-speed, low-latency, concurrent communications between PCI bus segments. Because RapidIO uses a direct memory-mapped model brought forward from Mercury's RACE® architecture, this implementation is easy to create. It offers particular advantages in PCI-X systems, where the number of devices that can be connected to the bus itself is very limited.

Latency and Determinism

Processors that are tightly connected to each other and to memory within a system require low latency. RapidIO addresses this requirement through the use of small packet headers and the absence of a software protocol stack.

In the RapidIO specification, packet headers are as small as possible to minimize the control overhead, and are organized for fast, efficient assembly and disassembly. Streaming writes, containing up to 256 bytes of data, require only 8 bytes of header information. Header size is constant regardless of the quantity of data in the packet, so efficiency increases with packet size up to the maximum data load of 256 bytes. At the same time, RapidIO's full duplex architecture eliminates delays as acknowledgements travel back to the sender.

Similar to the RACEway Interlink standard, multiple priority levels in RapidIO promote determinism by ensuring the most important transactions always get through. Each packet is assigned a transaction priority, with each priority associated with one of three transaction flows. Higher-priority flows take precedence over lower-priority flows, and RapidIO moves them before continuing with less-critical traffic. Within flows, all transactions complete on a first-come, first-served basis.

RapidIO is designed to work efficiently in heavily loaded networks. Because it can provide multiple paths between senders and recipients, packets can utilize adaptive routing to bypass busy channels. This allows the interconnection fabric to find alternate paths for blocked traffic, thereby decreasing the likelihood that data will be delayed due to conflicts for the route.

Bringing RapidIO to Market

As one of the driving forces behind RapidIO, Mercury's architects and engineers are already well attuned to this emerging standard. However, because the RapidIO Trade Association will be open to all interested parties, many companies are likely to bring RapidIO products to many different markets. High-end embedded digital signal and image processing applications will especially benefit from the increased performance and communication density that RapidIO provides. In fact, some of these solutions are likely to look very much like today's RACE architecture, even those from Mercury's competitors.

Ownership of the resulting specification will be transferred to the RapidIO Trade Association, open to all interested parties, once the group is formally established. As the embedded processing industry unites around RapidIO, competing architectures will no longer be impediments to users attempting to leverage the best technologies regardless of their source.

The expected broad adoption and availability of RapidIO interfaces means that customers will benefit from expanded choice and increased interoperability of components. Vendors too will be freed to innovate in areas such as processing density and optimization rather than developing expensive proprietary solutions.
